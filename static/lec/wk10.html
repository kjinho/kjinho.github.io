Written Advocacy

Features of Effective Advocacy

  • Humanize your client and cause
  • Keep your arguments clear and concise
  • Use roadmaps to orient your audience
  • Be honest
  • Comply with applicable rules

Motion Practice

  • Motion: a request for the court to take a specific action
  • Components:
    • motion
    • proposed order
    • brief/memorandum
    • evidence (affidavits, exhibits)

Common Motions

  • Motion to Dismiss
  • Motion to Compel (Production of Discovery)
  • Motion for Judgment
    • Judgment on the Pleadings
    • Summary Judgment
    • Judgment of Acquittal
    • Judgment Nothwithstanding the Verdict
  • Motion in Limine

Motion for Judgment

  • Typically based on all of the relevant, available facts
  • Typically resolve factual disputes in favor of the non-moving party
  • Relief: Judgment in the moving-party’s favor

Introduction

  • (Sometimes unlabeled, sometimes called “preliminary statement”)
  • Identifies parties and the relief sought
  • Establishes theme

Background

  • (Sometimes labeled Statement of Facts and/or Procedural History)
  • Continues the theme established in the introduction
  • Narrates the facts
  • Uses chronological order
  • DOES NOT ARGUE (avoid legal conclusions)

Argument

  • Uses IRAC
  • Uses persuasive headings
  • Persuades using facts and law, not (just) rhetoric
  • AVOIDS HOSTILITY AND SARCASM

Conclusion

Concisely states the relief requested

Summary Judgment Standard (example)

The Court should deny Dollar Depot’s grant summary judgment in favor of Dollar Depot. Under Texas law, a court should grant a motion for summary judgment if the moving party carries the burden of establishing that “there is no genuine issue as to any material fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” Tex. R. Civ. P. 166a(c); M.D. Anderson Hosp. & Tumor Inst. v. Willrich, 28 S.W.3d 22, 23 (Tex. 2000). In reviewing the facts, a court must consider them in the light most favorable to the non-moving party. Great Am. Rsrv. Ins. Co. v. San Antonio Plumbing Supply Co., 391 S.W.2d 41, 47 (Tex. 1965). Here, even taking the facts in the light most favorable to Groner, Dollar Depot is entitled to judgment as a matter of law because the evidence establishes the shopkeeper’s privilege. ...

Grading Criteria

  1. Overall Appearance
    1. Does the memo include all necessary sections?
    2. Is the typing and physical presentation neat and in the necessary format?
    3. Does the memo comply with the mandated word-count limit?
  2. Introduction
    1. Does the introduction identify the relief requested?
    2. Does the introduction establish the memo’s theme?
  3. Background
    1. Does the writer organize the factual narrative appropriately, such as by using an introductory paragraph?
    2. Does the writer present the facts in chronological order?
    3. Does the writer identify the parties and the nature of the dispute?
    4. Does the writer reconcile potential factual disputes appropriately?
    5. Has the writer included all material facts?
  4. Argument
    1. Does the argument begin with appropriate introductory paragraphs?
    2. Does the argument end with appropriate concluding paragraphs?
    3. Does each paragraph have a topic sentence?
    4. Are the paragraphs too long (more than 2/3 or 3/4 page)?
    5. Does the writer use the IRAC method consistently, setting forth rules, explaining the cases, applying the rules, and coming to a conclusion for each issue?
    6. Does the writer analyze the problem thoroughly?
    7. Does the writer compare specific case facts to the specific facts of the problem?
    8. Does the writer cite to authority whenever necessary?
  5. Style
    1. Did the writer review the paper for spelling, grammar, and typing errors?
    2. Do the citations comply with Bluebook form?
    3. Does the writer use the appropriate tone (avoiding hostility and sarcasm)?
    4. Does the writer use a consistent theme (or themes) throughout the memo?
  6. Conclusion
    1. Does the writer state the relief requested clearly and concisely?
