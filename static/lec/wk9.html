1. Narratives Revisited

1.1. Organizing Narratives

1.2. Introduce the matter

  • Nature of the dispute
  • Source of the facts, if appropriate

1.3. Introduce the parties and any necessary background information

  • Relationships between the parties, if appropriate
  • Setting information, if appropriate

1.4. Present the facts in chronological order, including any disputed facts

  • Highlight disputed allegations with word choice: (e.g., “Doe claims…,” “Moe alleges”)
  • Consider using a series of short, simple sentences to highlight important facts (e.g., “Wilkins sustained severe injuries from the accident. His right leg was broken in three places. He had three broken ribs. He had a compound fracture in his left arm. He had contusions along his back.”

1.5. Factual Persuasion

  • Use information to persuade
  • Avoid adverbs

2. Complaints

2.1. Purpose of the Complaint

  • Put the other party (and the court) on notice of the claim
  • Frame the matter so that it can survive dismissal

2.2. Contents of the Complaint

Fed. R. Civ. P. 8(a):

  1. “a short and plain statement of the grounds for the court’s jurisdiction, unless the court already has jurisdiction and the claim needs no new jurisdictional support;”
  2. “a short and plain statement of the claim showing that the pleader is entitled to relief; and”
  3. “a demand for the relief sought, which may include relief in the alternative or different types of relief.”

2.3. Stating a Claim

A “complaint must contain sufficient factual matter, accepted as true, to ‘state a claim to relief that is plausible on its face.’”1

2.4. Facts are the Basis of the Complaint

  • Advocacy through narrative
  • Themes

2.5. Stating a Claim

  • The facts must satisfy the elements of a legal claim
  • Requires legal research!

2.6. Plausibility

  • the facts must allow “the court to draw the reasonable inference that the defendant is liable for the misconduct alleged”2
  • the facts must offer “more than a sheer possibility that a defendant has acted unlawfully”3

3. Anatomy of the Complaint

3.1. Caption

  • Properly state the court/jurisdiction
  • Name the parties
  • Identify the document (“Complaint”)

3.2. Introduction

  • Summarize the nature of the dispute
  • Establish themes

3.3. Parties, Jurisdiction, Venue

State facts related to

  • the identity of the parties
  • the basis for the court’s jurisdiction
  • the basis for the venue

3.4. Factual Allegations

“A party must state its claims or defenses in numbered paragraphs, each limited as far as practicable to a single set of circumstances.”4

3.5. Claims of Relief

  • Identify the legal claims (and source of the law)
  • Briefly organize the previously stated facts to satisfy each element of the legal claim

3.6. Prayer for Relief

  • State clearly and concisely what remedies you want the court to grant
  • General damages do not need to be stated with specificity, but “special damages” do need specificity5
    • General damages: “all damages which are the natural or necessary consequences of the cause of action”6
    • Special damages: “damages not necessarily flowing from the acts set out in the declaration, and of which the defendant could not be supposed to have notice unless they were properly averred”7

3.7. Jury Demand

  • in many jurisdictions, failure to make a jury demand waives the right to a trial by jury8
  • local rules may require the jury demand to appear in a certain manner

3.8. Signature

  • Rules dictate what must be included, e.g., signer’s address, e-mail address, and telephone number9

4. Ethical Considerations

4.1. Accuracy of Pleadings

  • No false statements of fact or law
    • ABA Model R. Prof. C. 3.3: Candor Toward the Tribunal
    • ABA Model R. Prof. C. 3.4: Fairness to Opposing Party & Counsel
  • Reasonable inquiry (Fed. R. Civ. P. 11(a))
    • proper purpose
    • claims warranted under existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for extending, modifying, or reversing existing law or for establishing new law;
    • the factual contentions have evidentiary support or, if specifically so identified, will likely have evidentiary support after a reasonable opportunity for further investigation

5. Assignment

Assignment: Complaint
Grade: 5% of final grade
Deadline: March 30, 2022, at 6PM
Word Limit: 800
Special Requirements: Track hours spent on assignment

5.1. Grading Criteria

  • Overall Appearance
    • [ ] Does the complaint include all necessary sections?
    • [ ] Is the typing and physical presentation neat and in the necessary format?
    • [ ] Does the complaint comply with the mandated word-count limit?
  • Introduction
    • [ ] Does the introduction summarize the nature of the dispute?
    • [ ] Does the introduction establish the complaint’s themes?
  • Factual Allegations
    • [ ] Does the writer organize the factual narrative appropriately?
    • [ ] Does the writer present the facts in chronological order?
    • [ ] Does the writer identify the parties and the nature of the dispute?
    • [ ] Has the writer included all material facts?
  • Claims
    • [ ] Does the complaint make clear the legal basis for each claim?
    • [ ] Does the writer show how the facts satisfy the elements of each claim?
  • Prayer for Relief
    • [ ] Does the writer request appropriate relief?
  • Style
    • [ ] Did the writer review the paper for spelling, grammar, and typing errors?
    • [ ] Do the citations comply with Bluebook form?
    • [ ] Does the writer use the appropriate tone (avoiding hostility and sarcasm)?
    • [ ] Does the writer use a consistent theme (or themes) throughout the memo?

    To: Law Clerk
    From: Bond Forger, Partner
    Subject: Damian Desmond
    Date: March 23, 2022

    Damian Desmond recently hired our firm to represent him in a potential civil action against the state. As you may know, Mr. Desmond served more than twenty years in prison for a crime that he did not commit. Mr. Desmond is now 40 years old. When he entered prison, he was only 18. The world is dramatically different now, and Mr. Desmond would benefit from resources that will allow him to lead a meaningful life as a free man.

    Another associate has found that the Massachusetts Erroneous Felony Conviction Statute (Mass. Gen. Laws ch. 256D, §§ 1 et seq.) offers one potential legal basis for a claim against the state.

    Please draft a complaint we can file to initiate a lawsuit against the Commonwealth of Massachusetts under the Erroneous Felony Conviction Statute.

  3. Order of the Superior Court
    \begin{centering} COMMONWEALTH OF MASSACHUSETTS \end{centering}
    Suffolk, ss. \hspace{2in} Superior Court Department
        No. 2000-CR-12034
    \begin{centering} COMMONWEALTH v. DAMIAN DESMOND \underline{ORDER GRANTING MOTION FOR NEW TRIAL} \end{centering}

    This case arises from the stabbing death of Yuri Briar during the early morning hours of February 12, 2000. The murder was a grisly affair, involving a lengthy struggle between the victim and the murderer, who fled shortly after the victim’s death. The police could not immediately identify the culprit, but descriptions of the culprit led police to arrest the Defendant Damian Desmond a few days later. An eyewitness subsequently identified Desmond in a photo array.

    The testimony of the eyewitness formed the centerpiece of the Commonwealth’s prosecution of Desmond as the murderer. A jury of this Court found Desmond guilty of murder in the first degree, and this Court entered a judgment of conviction, sentencing Desmond to life imprisonment in the state prison in Walpole. The Supreme Judicial Court affirmed the conviction.

    After the legislature’s 2012 enactment of an Act Providing Access to Forensic and Scientific Analysis, Desmond began seeking further review of his case. Those efforts bore fruit. As outlined in the affidavits submitted to this Court, DNA analysis of the blood collected at the scene of the attack and on the knife conclusively excludes Desmond as a potential source of the DNA. Based on this newly discovered evidence, Desmond and the Commonwealth now jointly request that the Court vacate judgment of conviction.

    Although the Court has questions as to why the Commonwealth never analyzed the DNA evidence before, it sees no reason to delay Desmond’s release.

    The joint motion of the parties being before it, the Court hereby ALLOWS the motion and vacates the conviction of Defendant Damian Desmond for murder in the first degree.

    \underline{/s/Sylvia Sherwood}
    Sylvia Sherwood, J.
    March 1, 2022
