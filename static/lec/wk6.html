Questions Presented

135
The question presented fails to identify the relevant legal theory, fails to include facts for context, and is unanswerable “yes” or “no.”The question presented identifies a relevant legal theory, includes factual context, and is answerable “yes” or “no.” The legal theory lacks sufficient specificity. The included facts provide some context but fail to individuate the issue.The question presented identifies the relevant legal theory with precision, includes case-specific facts that give the question helpful context, and is answerable “yes” or “no.”

Questions Presented: Tips

  • Distinguish between the “legal” issue and the “business”/“personal” issue.
  • A single question presented should include both facts and law.
  • Consider using the “under”/“does”/“when” form: “Under California Civil Code section 3344, does a celebrity have a cause of action for violation of his right of publicity when a business uses his photograph in an advertisement without permission?”

Questions Presented: Example for Discussion

Can Richards prove “undue hardship” under § 523(a)(8) of the Bankruptcy Code?

135

The writer has omitted critically important facts and has included irrelevant and unnecessary information. There is little or no recognizable organization of the facts.The statement of facts contains most of what a reader needs to understand the factual circumstance of the matter. The writer identifies the parties and nature of the dispute. The writer includes most of the important facts and has omitted most irrelevant or unnecessary information. The writer includes a brief introductory paragraph that identifies the parties and the nature of the dispute. The writer organizes the facts clearly and in chronological order. Discussion: High-Level Organization

135
The writer fails to use IRAC correctly to organize the discussion. The discussion lacks an introductory paragraph. The writer fails to begin each paragraph with a topic sentence. The discussion lacks an appropriate concluding sentence or paragraph.The writer uses IRAC to organize the discussion. The discussion begins with an introductory paragraph that either fails to define the issue, fails to state the correct issue, or fails to state the applicable rule. The introductory paragraph is missing a “roadmap,” or the roadmap that it provides does not match the discussion that follows. Some, but not all, of the paragraphs in the discussion begin with a topic sentence. The discussion lacks an appropriate concluding sentence or paragraph that terminates the IRAC that began in the discussion’s introductory paragraph.The writer uses IRAC in a clear, consistent way to organize the discussion. The discussion begins with an introductory paragraph that states the issue and the applicable rule. The introductory paragraph contains a “roadmap” to orient the reader on the analysis that follows. Each paragraph in the discussion begins with a topic sentence. The discussion ends with an appropriate concluding sentence or paragraph that terminates the IRAC that began in the discussion’s introductory paragraph.

Discussion: Element-Level Organization

135
The writer fails to include a separate IRAC for each legal element that requires analysis.The writer includes a separate IRAC for each legal element that requires analysis. In the IRAC for most of the elements discussed, the writer states the issue and the rule, explains the case law, applies the rule, and and concisely sums up the element in a concluding sentence or sentences. The writer occasionally mixes the order of the “Rule” and “Application” in IRAC, but the ideas expressed still come across clearly.The writer includes a separate IRAC for each legal element that requires analysis. The writer sequences the IRACs in a logical way. For each element discussed, the writer—in this order—states the issue and the rule, explains the case law, applies the rule, and sums up the analysis in a concluding sentence or sentences.

Discussion: Analysis

135
The writer only performs a cursory analysis and ignores many of the important facts. The writer fails to compare the case law and the problem facts to explain the outcome of the analysis. The writer fails to make the source of authority clear.The writer analyzes the problem well, addressing many of the important facts. The writer compares the case law and the problem facts to explain the outcome of the analysis, but the comparisons unnecessarily rely more on distinctions than similarities. The writer makes the source of all authority clear, but the discussion is still missing some citations or pinpoint citations where required.The writer analyzes the problem thoroughly. The writer addresses all of the facts, organized under appropriate legal elements. The writer relies on positive comparisons between the case law and the problem facts to explain why the outcome of the analysis is similar to the case-law outcome. The writer distinguishes inapposite case law but does not rely excessively on differences between the case law and the problem facts to explain why the outcome of the analysis must be different from the case-law outcome. The writer cites to authority, including pinpoint citations, wherever necessary.

Discussion: Tips

  • “Pyramid” theory
    1. Assume an ignorant reader.
    2. To help the reader understand, you need to build a strong foundation.
    3. Everything you write needs to be built on law and facts you’ve already explained.
  • Element-Level Organization
    1. For simple elements, a single paragraph containing IRAC can suffice.
    2. For most elements, I recommend splitting IRAC into at least two paragraphs:
      1. Paragraph 1: Issue and Rule
      2. Paragraph 2: Application and Conclusion
    3. Remember that each paragraph must begin with a topic sentence.

Analysis: Example for Discussion

Second, additional circumstances exist indicating that
Mr. Richards’s situation is likely to persist for a sizable
portion of the repayment period of the student loans. In
Cheesman, the debtors’ financial situation placed the family
finances in a deficit if they paid the student loan.
Cheesman, 25 F.3d at 360. In Ekenasi, the student loans
allowed the debtor to commence a career in law that improved his
circumstances. Ekenasi, 325 F.3d at 548. In the present case,
Mr. Richards will enter a deficit of $250 dollars if he pays the
student loan. Moreover, a health expert has advised him to not
commence a career in law because of the severe depression that has
been diagnosed. Therefore, it is likely that Mr. Richards’s
circumstances will persist for a sizable portion of the repayment
period of the student loans.

Style: Tips

  1. Keep your sentences short!
  2. Definite and indefinite articles (“a,” “an,” “the”): Bouchoux 282-83.
  3. Punctuation: Commas and periods go inside the quotation marks: Bluebook R5.1(b)(iv).

Citations: Example for Discussion

In Cheesman v. Tennessee Student Assistance Corp., the Sixth
Circuit found that the Cheesmans could not maintain a minimal
standard of living for their family if they were required to repay
their loans. See Cheesman v. Tenn. Student Assistance
Corp. (In re Cheesman), 25 F.3d 356, 359 (6th Cir. 1994). The
Cheesmans’ 1992 gross income exceeded by only a slim margin the
government’s 1992 poverty income guideline. Id. And based on
their current situation and employment history, there was no
assurance that their financial condition would improve in the
foreseeable future. Id. at 360. This was one of the main
reasons why the court held that the Cheesmans student loans were
dischargeable. Id. at 359–60.
