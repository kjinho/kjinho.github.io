2.4. Features of Closing Arguments

  • Using simple language that the jury can understand
  • Reconciling facts and witness credibility
  • Explaining weaknesses
  • Applying the law

2.5. Organization

  • Introduction: Themes, overview of the facts
  • State the law
  • Explain the significant of the facts under the law (TREAT)

2.6. Ethical Considerations

  • No false statements of fact or law1
  • No statements that the lawyer “does not reasonably believe is relevant”2
  • No statements that “will not be supported by admissible evidence”3
  • No statements that assert “personal knowledge”4
  • No statements that state personal opinion as to the “justness of a cause, credibility of a witness, the culpability of a civil litigant or the guilt or innocence of an accused”5

3.1. Grading Criteria

  • Overall Appearance
    • [ ] Is the typing and physical presentation neat and in the necessary format?
    • [ ] Does the complaint comply with the mandated word-count limit?
  • Argument (Introduction)
    • [ ] Does the introduction summarize the nature of the dispute?
    • [ ] Does the introduction establish the argument’s themes?
  • Argument (Persuasion)
    • [ ] Does the argument make proper use of the jury instructions?
    • [ ] Does the writer show how the facts satisfy the elements of the claim?
    • [ ] Does the writer present a persuasive argument?
  • Style
    • [ ] Did the writer review the paper for spelling, grammar, and typing errors?
    • [ ] Does the writer use the appropriate tone (avoiding hostility and sarcasm)?
    • [ ] Does the writer use a consistent theme (or themes) throughout the argument?

To: Law Clerk
From: Marissa Clarke, Partner
Subject: Sidney Young
Date: March 30, 2022

This firm represents Sidney Young. As you know, Mr. Young was recently involved in a serious single-car accident that caused him serious injuries.

The liability phase of the trial has just concluded. Please draft a closing argument to the jury to explain why the jury should find Mr. Gardner liable for Mr. Young’s injuries.

I have enclosed a copy of the judge’s instructions to the jury, as well as copies of the trial transcript.

3.2.2. Jury Charge (Instructions)

  1. Burden of Proof

    When a person files a civil case, he has the burden of proof. That means that he must present enough evidence to prove his claims. What is enough evidence?

    I will tell you in a minute each of the specific things that Sidney Young must prove. Sidney Young must show that each of these things is more likely true than not true. The lawyers or I may use the phrase “more probable than not,” and that means the same thing as “more likely true than not true.”

    Now, from time to time, I will refer to proof or to proving something. By this, I mean showing that Sidney Young’s version of the facts is more likely true than not true, based upon evidence and any reasonable conclusions from the evidence.

    If you find that the evidence supporting Sidney Young’s version of the facts is more persuasive, then you must decide in favor of Sidney Young. But if you find that the evidence supporting Riley Gardner’s version of the facts is more persuasive, or that the evidence on the two sides is equally persuasive—50/50—then you must decide in favor of Riley Gardner.

  2. Credibility

    A very important part of your job as jurors is to decide who and what to believe. You may believe everything a witness says, part of it, or none of it.

    Sometimes people do not tell the truth. You must decide whether a witness was being truthful, or was deliberately lying. If you conclude that a witness lied to you about something, then of course you should not believe that part of the testimony.

    Sometimes people make an honest mistake. You must also decide whether a witness testified accurately, or whether the witness may have gotten something wrong without meaning to do so. A witness may recall seeing or hearing something, but actually be mistaken.

    For example, the witness might not have paid close attention or might have misunderstood what was happening. Or a witness’s memory of what happened could be incorrect.

    If you conclude that a witness tried to be truthful but that some part of the person’s testimony was not accurate, then you should not consider the inaccurate testimony.

    How do you decide whether you believe particular statements by a witness? Ask yourself:

    • Did the testimony seem reasonable or probable?
    • Did the witness have a good chance to observe what happened? How much attention did the witness pay?
    • Does the witness’s memory seem accurate?
    • Was the witness’s testimony consistent with other evidence, or instead contradicted by other evidence?
    • How did the witness behave and answer questions?
    • Did the witness have any motive for testifying in a certain way or have any other bias that may have influenced the witness’s testimony?
  3. Negligence

    Sidney Young claims that Riley Garnder was negligent in operating a motor vehicle. The fact that Sidney Young has filed this lawsuit is not evidence that Riley Gardner was negligent or caused any injuries. To prove negligence, Sidney Young must show that the following three things are more likely true than not true:

    1. That Riley Gardner failed to use reasonable care under all of the circumstances; and
    2. That Riley Gardner’s failure to use reasonable care was a cause of Sidney Young’s injury or harm.

    I will now describe each of these things in more detail.

  4. Breach of Duty

    First, Sidney Young must prove that Riley Gardner more likely than not failed to use reasonable care under the circumstances. That failure might have occurred through action or inaction. You must consider all the evidence and then make a judgment about what a reasonably careful person would have done.

    You should consider what Riley Gardner did or failed to do. Then you should consider all of the relevant circumstances. For example, you may ask the following questions:

    • Where, when and how did the incident occur?
    • How likely was it that people could be injured?
    • How serious was the potential injury?
    • How obvious should the risk have been to DFT?
    • Was there any emergency?
    • How much of a burden would it have been to avoid the injury?

    Then you make a judgment. The law requires a person to use reasonable care to avoid predictable injuries to people. We ask you, the jury, to consider what a reasonable and careful person would have done under these circumstances. What you consider reasonable may vary depending on the circumstances of this case. A person should take greater precautions as the risk of harm increases. You should also ask yourself: “Did Riley Gardner actually know about any risks?” And, if not: “Should he have known about them?”

    A driver of a motor vehicle must act as a reasonably careful person would act in similar circumstances. A driver must use reasonable care:

    • to avoid placing himself/herself or others in danger,
    • to keep a proper lookout,
    • to follow all traffic signs and signals,
    • to make reasonable observations about traffic and weather conditions,
    • to obey all motor vehicle laws,
    • to maintain control of his/her vehicle and not be distracted, and
    • to avoid a collision.

    As harm becomes more likely, the driver must be more careful. For example, a driver must be more careful when driving in an area full of pedestrians than in an area where no pedestrians are present. And as the threatened harm becomes more severe, the driver must be more careful. For example, someone driving at a high speed near other vehicles needs to be more careful than someone driving very slowly when no one else is around.

    The fact that a collision occurred is not itself evidence of negligence. However, you can and should consider how a collision occurred when you are determining whether Riley Gardner was negligent.

    Whether Riley Gardner used reasonable care under the circumstances is for you, the jury, to determine. If Riley Gardner did not use reasonable care under the circumstances, then you should find that he was negligent. If Riley Gardner did use reasonable care under the circumstances, then you should find that he was not negligent.

  5. Causation

    As I mentioned earlier, the second thing that Sidney Young must prove is that, more likely than not, Riley Gardner’s negligence caused Sidney Young’s injuries. You must ask: “Would the same harm have happened without Riley Gardner’s negligence?” In other words, did the negligence make a difference? If Riley Gardner’s negligence had an impact on Sidney Young’s injuries, then it caused those injuries. But if the negligence had no impact on Sidney Young’s injuries and the same harm would have happened anyway, then Riley Gardner did not cause the injuries.

    Often, an injury has more than one cause. If Riley Gardner’s negligence was one of those causes, that is enough. Sidney Young does not have to show that Riley Gardner’s negligence was the only cause of the injuries. Nor does he have to show that the negligence was the largest or main cause of the injuries, as long as the injuries would not have occurred without Riley Gardner’s negligence.
