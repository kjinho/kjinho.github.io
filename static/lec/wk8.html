Pinpoint Citation

Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Resendez, 962 S.W.2d 539, 539-40 (Tex.
1998).

This case involves the proper scope of authority
of law in a false imprisonment case. The court of appeals
affirmed the trial court’s judgment against Wal–Mart Stores,
Inc. (“Wal–Mart”) on a jury finding of false imprisonment.–––
S.W.2d––––. We hold that, on the facts in this case, Wal–Mart
established as a matter of law that it detained Lucia Resendez
for a reasonable period of time, in a reasonable manner, and
upon a reasonable belief that she had stolen store
merchandise. Accordingly, we reverse the *540 court of
appeals’ judgment and render judgment for Wal–Mart.

On January 20, 1986, Resendez went shopping at Wal–Mart
during her lunch break. While browsing through the store, she
began to eat from a bag of peanuts marked with a Wal–Mart
price sticker. Raul Salinas, a security guard for Wal–Mart,
followed Resendez and observed her place the empty bag under a
rose bush. He then watched her purchase some items and leave
the store. After determining that Resendez had not paid for a
bag of peanuts, Salinas followed her into the parking lot. He
accused her of taking the bag of peanuts without paying and
asked her to accompany him back into the store. Resendez
objected that she bought the peanuts the day before at another
Wal–Mart store and could provide the receipt to prove it. She
then accompanied Salinas to the back of the store. Within
about ten to fifteen minutes a police officer arrived and
arrested Resendez. Resendez posted bail and was released about
one hour later.

Client Letter

  • Who is the audience?
  • What is the purpose?
For more information, see chapter 6 of the Bouchoux textbook.

Parts of the Client Letter

  • Letterhead
  • Date
  • Special Notations
  • Inside Address
  • Reference/Subject
  • Salutation
  • Body
  • Closing
  • Signature Block
  • Reference Initials, Enclosures, Copies

Letterhead

  • Contact Information
  • Branding/Marketing
  • Only on First Page

Date

  • Signals the “freshness” of the opinion
  • Use “full” date format: July 4, 1776

Special Notations

  • Hand Delivered, Registered Mail, By Email
  • Attorney-Client Communication—Privileged and Confidential

Inside Address

  • Name and Address of Recipient
  • Note: Do not use “Mrs.” for female recipients

Reference/Subject

  • Include matter name and reference numbers, where applicable

Salutation

  • Formal!
  • “Dear Ms. Smith:”
  • “Dear Sir or Madam:”

Body

  • Introduction
  • Statement of Facts
  • Opinion
  • Explanation of Opinion
  • Recommendations
  • Instructions
  • Restrictions/Protection Clauses

Protection Clause

“We have based this opinion upon the facts you have provided us....”

Closing

  • “Please do not hesitate to contact me with any questions or concerns.”

Signature Block

  • “Sincerely,”
  • “Very truly yours,”
  • “Best regards,”
  • “Respectfully,”

Reference Initials, Enclosures, Copies

  • “LEM/mjs” means Leonard E. Milligan is the author and Michael J. Sharkey is the typist.
  • “Encl.” means the sender has enclosed something with the letter.
  • “cc:” means the sender is sending copies to the named individuals.

Client Letter Assignment

  • Write an opinion letter to the client Natasha Romanova
  • Word limit: 800 words
    • You may exclude from your word limit the letterhead, date, special notations, inside address, reference/subject, the signature block, and anything that follows the signature block.

Special Considerations for Email

  • How is a typical email different from a letter?
  • Treat professional emails like letters

Is email appropriate?

  • Audience
  • Formality
  • Confidentiality

Subject Line

  • Purpose
  • Client code / case name / issue
  • Ability to locate email

Tone

  • Business professional
  • No informal internet acronyms (LOL, FYI, BTW, etc.)
  • Proper salutation and close
  • CAPS convey anger

Confidentiality

  • Subject to discovery
  • Confidentiality statements
  • Limits to confidentiality

Email signatures

  • Contact Information
  • Confidentiality Notice

Attachments

  • Refer and describe in text
  • Check they can be opened
  • Name of attachment

Proofread

  • “Reply all”
  • Email chains
  • Distribution lists / recipients

Strategies to Achieve Brevity

  • Minor
    • Eliminate the passive voice
    • Rewrite long sentences
  • Major
    • Identify the important ideas
    • Rewrite to keep only the important ideas
    • Reorganize

Client Letter Grading Rubric

Overall Appearance

135
The document fails to include all necessary sections. The document is not in the appropriate format.The document fails to comply with the mandated word-count limit, but it includes all necessary sections. The document is in the appropriate format, but there are inconsistencies or errors in the formatting.The document complies with the mandated word-count limit and includes all necessary sections. The writer has formatted the document consistently and appropriately.

Introduction

135
The introduction fails to identify the issue, fails to include facts for context, and fails to give sufficient context for the letter’s purpose.The introduction identifies the issue, includes factual context, and states the letter’s purpose. The legal theory lacks sufficient specificity or relies excessively on legalese. The included facts provide some context but fail to individuate the issue.The introduction identifies the relevant issue with precision, includes case-specific facts that give the issue helpful context, and clearly establishes the purpose of the letter.

Statement of Facts

135
The statement of facts fails to contain what a reader needs to understand the factual circumstance of the matter. It is unclear who the parties are. It is unclear what the nature of the dispute is. The writer has omitted critically important facts and has included irrelevant and unnecessary information. There is little or no recognizable organization of the facts. There is no indication of the source of the facts.The statement of facts contains most of what a reader needs to understand the factual circumstance of the matter. The writer identifies the parties and nature of the dispute. The writer includes most of the important facts and has omitted most irrelevant or unnecessary information. The organization of the facts is comprehensible, although the writer could improve the organization by using chronological order. The letter states the source of the facts.The statement of facts contains everything a reader needs to understand the factual circumstance of the matter. The writer includes a brief introductory paragraph that identifies the parties and the nature of the dispute. The writer organizes the facts clearly and in chronological order. The writer includes all facts that he or she later relies on in the discussion and omits irrelevant and unnecessary information. The source of the facts is clear.

Opinion

135
The letter fails to state an opinion responsive to the issue.The letter states an opinion, but the opinion is either unclear or difficult to locate within the letter.The letter states a clear opinion responsive to the issue. The letter uses the language of probability to frame the opinion.

Explanation of Opinion (Discussion)

135
The writer fails to use IRAC correctly to organize the discussion. The writer fails to begin each paragraph with a topic sentence. The discussion lacks an appropriate concluding sentence or paragraph. The writer fails to include a separate IRAC for each legal element that requires analysis.The writer uses IRAC to organize the discussion. The introductory paragraph is missing a “roadmap,” or the roadmap that it provides does not match the discussion that follows. Some, but not all, of the paragraphs in the discussion begin with a topic sentence. The discussion lacks an appropriate concluding sentence or paragraph that terminates the IRAC that began in the discussion’s introductory paragraph. The writer includes a separate IRAC for each legal element that requires analysis.The writer uses IRAC in a clear, consistent way to organize the discussion. Each paragraph in the discussion begins with a topic sentence. The discussion ends with an appropriate concluding sentence or paragraph that terminates the IRAC that began in the discussion’s introductory paragraph. The writer includes a separate IRAC for each legal element that requires analysis. For each element discussed, the writer—in this order—states the issue and the rule, explains the rule, applies the rule, and sums up the analysis in a concluding sentence or sentences.

Recommendations and Instructions

135
The conclusion fails to answer the issue. The conclusion lacks questions or recommendations for the reader.The conclusion answers all issue. The conclusion also contains questions, recommendations, or instructions for the reader. These questions or recommendations are either minimally relevant or largely unhelpful.The conclusion answers all issues clearly and succinctly. The conclusion also contains relevant and helpful questions, recommendations, or instructions for the reader.

Writing Style

135
The writer uses sentences that are unnecessarily complex or compound. The writer uses the passive voice repeatedly. The writer repeatedly uses incorrect or inappropriates words. Several paragraphs are too short or too long. The document contains numerous spelling, grammar, or typing errors.The writer uses mostly simple, subject-verb-object sentences, although some sentences are unnecessarily complex or compound. The writer uses the passive voice more than once in the document. The writer uses correct, but unnecessarily complex words. The paragraphs are mostly of appropriate length (normally at least 2 or 3 sentences long, and no longer than three-quarters of a page). The document contains few spelling, grammar, or typing errors.The writer uses simple, subject-verb-object sentences. The writer uses the active voice and avoids the passive voice. The writer uses simple, familiar words. The writer avoids the overuse of prepositions and prepositional phrases. The paragraphs are of appropriate length (normally at least 2 or 3 sentences long, and no longer than three-quarters of a page). The document is free of spelling, grammar, and typing errors.
