Pinpoint Citation

Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. v. Resendez, 962 S.W.2d 539, 539-40 (Tex.
1998).

This case involves the proper scope of authority
of law in a false imprisonment case. The court of appeals
affirmed the trial court’s judgment against Wal–Mart Stores,
Inc. (“Wal–Mart”) on a jury finding of false imprisonment.–––
S.W.2d––––. We hold that, on the facts in this case, Wal–Mart
established as a matter of law that it detained Lucia Resendez
for a reasonable period of time, in a reasonable manner, and
upon a reasonable belief that she had stolen store
merchandise. Accordingly, we reverse the *540 court of
appeals’ judgment and render judgment for Wal–Mart.

On January 20, 1986, Resendez went shopping at Wal–Mart
during her lunch break. While browsing through the store, she
began to eat from a bag of peanuts marked with a Wal–Mart
price sticker. Raul Salinas, a security guard for Wal–Mart,
followed Resendez and observed her place the empty bag under a
rose bush. He then watched her purchase some items and leave
the store. After determining that Resendez had not paid for a
bag of peanuts, Salinas followed her into the parking lot. He
accused her of taking the bag of peanuts without paying and
asked her to accompany him back into the store. Resendez
objected that she bought the peanuts the day before at another
Wal–Mart store and could provide the receipt to prove it. She
then accompanied Salinas to the back of the store. Within
about ten to fifteen minutes a police officer arrived and
arrested Resendez. Resendez posted bail and was released about
one hour later.

Client Letter

  • Who is the audience?
  • What is the purpose?
For more information, see chapter 6 of the Bouchoux textbook.

Parts of the Client Letter

  • Letterhead
  • Date
  • Special Notations
  • Inside Address
  • Reference/Subject
  • Salutation
  • Body
  • Closing
  • Signature Block
  • Reference Initials, Enclosures, Copies

Letterhead

  • Contact Information
  • Branding/Marketing
  • Only on First Page

Date

  • Signals the “freshness” of the opinion
  • Use “full” date format: July 4, 1776

Special Notations

  • Hand Delivered, Registered Mail, By Email
  • Attorney-Client Communication—Privileged and Confidential

Inside Address

  • Name and Address of Recipient
  • Note: Do not use “Mrs.” for female recipients

Reference/Subject

  • Include matter name and reference numbers, where applicable

Salutation

  • Formal!
  • “Dear Ms. Smith:”
  • “Dear Sir or Madam:”

Body

  • Introduction
  • Statement of Facts
  • Opinion
  • Explanation of Opinion
  • Recommendations
  • Instructions
  • Restrictions/Protection Clauses

Protection Clause

“We have based this opinion upon the facts you have provided us....”

Closing

  • “Please do not hesitate to contact me with any questions or concerns.”

Signature Block

  • “Sincerely,”
  • “Very truly yours,”
  • “Best regards,”
  • “Respectfully,”

Reference Initials, Enclosures, Copies

  • “LEM/mjs” means Leonard E. Milligan is the author and Michael J. Sharkey is the typist.
  • “Encl.” means the sender has enclosed something with the letter.
  • “cc:” means the sender is sending copies to the named individuals.

Special Considerations for Email

  • How is a typical email different from a letter?
  • Treat professional emails like letters

Is email appropriate?

  • Audience
  • Formality
  • Confidentiality

Subject Line

  • Purpose
  • Client code / case name / issue
  • Ability to locate email

Tone

  • Business professional
  • No informal internet acronyms (LOL, FYI, BTW, etc.)
  • Proper salutation and close
  • CAPS convey anger

Confidentiality

  • Subject to discovery
  • Confidentiality statements
  • Limits to confidentiality

Email signatures

  • Contact Information
  • Confidentiality Notice

Attachments

  • Refer and describe in text
  • Check they can be opened
  • Name of attachment

Proofread

  • “Reply all”
  • Email chains
  • Distribution lists / recipients

Strategies to Achieve Brevity

  • Minor
    • Eliminate the passive voice
    • Rewrite long sentences
  • Major
    • Identify the important ideas
    • Rewrite to keep only the important ideas
    • Reorganize
