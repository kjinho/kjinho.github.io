Office Memorandum (10 minutes)

  • Who is the audience?
  • What is its purpose?
  • What form does it take?

Bouchoux discusses the office memorandum at length.

Memorandum Grading Rubric

  1. Overall Appearance
  2. Questions Presented
  3. Brief Answer
  4. Statement of Facts
  5. Discussion: Organization
  6. Discussion: Analysis
  7. Conclusion
  8. Style

Overall Appearance

  1. Does the document include all necessary sections?
  2. Is the typing and physical presentation neat and in the necessary format?
  3. Does the document comply with the mandated word-count limit?

Questions Presented

  1. Does the writer ask questions that can be answered yes or no?
  2. Does the question identify the legal theory?
  3. Did the writer include the material facts?

Questions Presented: Tips

  • Distinguish between the “legal” issue and the “business”/“personal” issue.
  • A single question presented should include both facts and law.

Questions Presented: Example for Discussion

Can Ren prove “undue hardship” under § 523(a)(8) of the Bankruptcy Code?

Brief Answer

  1. Does the brief answer respond to the question presented?
  2. Does the writer include the material facts necessary to reach the conclusion?
  3. Does the writer include a rationale for the brief answer?

Brief Answer: Example for Discussion

No. Monthly payments of loans does not impose an “undue hardship” on Ren because he can maintain a minimal standard of living and his bad financial situation will change in foreseeable future.

Statement of Facts

  1. Does the writer identify the parties and the nature of the dispute?
  2. Does the writer present the facts in chronological order?
  3. Has the writer included all material facts, including all facts upon which the writer relies in the discussion, and omitted irrelevant or unnecessary information?

Discussion: Organization

  1. Does the discussion begin with an appropriate introductory paragraph?
  2. Does the discussion end with an appropriate conclusion?
  3. Does each paragraph have a topic sentence?
  4. Does the writer present the issues in a logical order?
  5. Does the writer use the IRAC method consistently, setting forth rules, explaining the cases, applying the rules, and coming to a conclusion for each issue?

Discussion: Analysis

  1. Does the writer analyze the problem thoroughly?
  2. Does the writer compare specific case facts to the specific facts of the problem?
  3. Does the writer cite to authority whenever necessary?

Analysis: Tips

  • “Pyramid” theory
    1. Assume an ignorant reader.
    2. To help the reader understand, you need to build a strong foundation.
    3. Everything you write needs to be built on law and facts you’ve already explained.

Analysis: Example for Discussion

Second, additional circumstances exist indicating that Mr. Ren’s
situation is likely to persist for a sizable portion of the
repayment period of the student loans. In Cheesman, the
debtors’ financial situation placed the family finances in a
deficit if they paid the student loan. Cheesman, 25 F.3d at
360. In Ekenasi, the student loans allowed the debtor to
commence a career in law that improved his circumstances.
Ekenasi, 325 F.3d at 548. In the present case, Mr. Ren will
enter a deficit of $250 dollars if he pays the student loan.
Moreover, a health expert has advised him to not commence a career
in law because of the severe depression that has been diagnosed.
Therefore, it is likely that Mr. Ren’s circumstances will persist
for a sizable portion of the repayment period of the student
loans.

Conclusion

  1. In the conclusion, does the writer answer all questions presented clearly and succinctly?
  2. Does the writer set forth questions, if any, and recommendations for the reader?

Style

  1. Does the writer use simple, subject-verb-object sentences?
  2. Does the writer use the active voice?
  3. Does the writer use simple, familiar words and avoid the overuse of prepositions?
  4. Are the paragraphs too long (more than 2/3 or 3/4 page)?
  5. Did the writer review the paper for spelling, grammar and typing errors?
  6. Do the citations comply with Bluebook form?

Style: Tips

  1. Keep your sentences short!
  2. Definite and indefinite articles (“a,” “an,” “the”): Bouchoux 282-83.
  3. Punctuation: Commas and periods go inside the quotation marks: Bluebook R5.1(b)(iv).

Citations: Example for Discussion

In Cheesman v. Tennessee Student Assistance Corp., the Sixth
Circuit found that the Cheesmans could not maintain a minimal
standard of living for their family if they were required to repay
their loans. See Cheesman v. Tenn. Student Assistance
Corp. (In re Cheesman), 25 F.3d 356, 359 (6th Cir. 1994). The
Cheesmans’ 1992 gross income exceeded by only a slim margin the
government’s 1992 poverty income guideline. Id. And based on
their current situation and employment history, there was no
assurance that their financial condition would improve in the
foreseeable future. Id. at 360. This was one of the main
reasons why the court held that the Cheesmans student loans were
dischargeable. Id. at 359–60.

Editing

  1. Focus on clarity.
  2. Eliminate the unnecessary.
