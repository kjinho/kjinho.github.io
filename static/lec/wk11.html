Written Advocacy

Features of Effective Advocacy

  • Humanize your client and cause
  • Keep your arguments clear and concise
  • Use roadmaps to orient your audience
  • Be honest
  • Comply with applicable rules

Oral Advocacy

  • Purpose: Assist a decision-maker to decide in your favor
  • Form: Presentation and Question Answering

Effective Oral Advocacy

  • Introduction
  • Roadmap
  • Primacy/Recency
  • Etiquette

Introduction

“May it please the Court. My name is ... , and I represent ....”

Roadmap

“We ask the Court to ... for three reasons. ...”

Primacy/Recency

  • start with strongest argument
  • put weaker arguments in the middle
  • end with a strong argument and a clear request

Etiqutte

  • judicial deference
  • “Your Honor,” “Judge,” “My Brother/Sister,” “Counsel”
  • answer questions directly

Brown v. BMP

  • Ms. Brown is a medical records clerk [for BMP] ....
  • About five minutes into her walk, Ms. Brown was knocked down by a young man learning how to skateboard. She broke her arm and dislocated her shoulder in the accident. She missed one month of work due to her injuries. Ms. Brown is still recovering and expects she will need physical therapy for the next three to six months.
  • Ms. Brown filed a workers’ compensation claim for lost wages and healthcare expenses. The issue before the court is whether she can recover under Alabama’s Workers’ Compensation Law.

Relevant Law

  • Ala. Code § 25-5-1 (1975)
  • “In order for the claimant’s injuries to be compensable under the Alabama Workmen’s Compensation laws, the accident must arise out of and occur in the course of his employment.” Sun Papers, Inc. v. Jerrell, 411 So. 2d 790, 793 (Ala. Civ. App. 1981).
  • “The phrase ‘arising out of’ an employee’s employment requires a causal connection between the injury and the employment. ... The phrase ‘in the course of’ the employee’s employment refers to the time, place, and circumstances under which the accident occurred.”
    Waffle House, Inc. v. Howard, 794 So. 2d 1123, 1128 (Ala. Civ. App. 2000).
  • “It is well settled that work-connected activity goes beyond the direct services performed for the employer and includes at least some ministration to the personal comfort and human wants of the employee. ... Such acts which are necessary to the life, comfort, and convenience of the employee while at work, though strictly personal to himself, and not acts of service are incidental to the service. Therefore, an injury sustained in the performance thereof is deemed to have arisen out of the employment.”
    Gold Kist, Inc. v. Jones, 537 So. 2d 39, 41 (Ala. Civ. App. 1988)

Argument: High-Level Organization

135
The writer fails to use IRAC correctly to organize the argument. The argument lacks an introductory paragraph. The writer fails to begin each paragraph with a topic sentence. The argument lacks an appropriate concluding sentence or paragraph.The writer uses IRAC to organize the argument. The argument begins with an introductory paragraph that either fails to define the issue, fails to state the correct issue, or fails to state the applicable rule. The introductory paragraph is missing a “roadmap,” or the roadmap that it provides does not match the argument that follows. Some, but not all, of the paragraphs in the argument begin with a topic sentence. The argument lacks an appropriate concluding sentence or paragraph that terminates the IRAC that began in the argument’s introductory paragraph.The writer uses IRAC in a clear, consistent way to organize the argument. The argument begins with an introductory paragraph that states the issue and the applicable rule. The introductory paragraph contains a “roadmap” to orient the reader on the analysis that follows. Each paragraph in the argument begins with a topic sentence. The argument ends with an appropriate concluding sentence or paragraph that terminates the IRAC that began in the argument’s introductory paragraph.

Argument: Element-Level Organization

135
The writer fails to include a separate IRAC for each legal element that requires analysis.The writer includes a separate IRAC for each legal element that requires analysis. In the IRAC for most of the elements discussed, the writer states the issue and the rule, explains the case law, applies the rule, and and concisely sums up the element in a concluding sentence or sentences. The writer occasionally mixes the order of the “Rule” and “Application” in IRAC, but the ideas expressed still come across clearly.The writer includes a separate IRAC for each legal element that requires analysis. The writer sequences the IRACs in a logical way. For each element discussed, the writer—in this order—states the issue and the rule, explains the case law, applies the rule, and sums up the analysis in a concluding sentence or sentences.

Argument: Analysis

135
The writer only performs a cursory analysis and ignores many of the important facts. The writer fails to compare the case law and the problem facts to explain the outcome of the analysis. The writer fails to make the source of authority clear.The writer analyzes the problem well, addressing many of the important facts. The writer compares the case law and the problem facts to explain the outcome of the analysis, but the comparisons unnecessarily rely more on distinctions than similarities. The writer makes the source of all authority clear, but the argument is still missing some citations or pinpoint citations where required.The writer analyzes the problem thoroughly. The writer addresses all of the facts, organized under appropriate legal elements. The writer relies on positive comparisons between the case law and the problem facts to explain why the outcome of the analysis is similar to the case-law outcome. The writer distinguishes inapposite case law but does not rely excessively on differences between the case law and the problem facts to explain why the outcome of the analysis must be different from the case-law outcome. The writer cites to authority, including pinpoint citations, wherever necessary.
